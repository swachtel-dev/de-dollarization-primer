De-dollarization Primer

The dollar's role in global reserves, the forces challenging it, and what alternatives are emerging.

Before you play the Reserve Currency Wargame, this primer gives you the factual foundation you need: what the dollar actually does in global finance, why countries are diversifying away from it, and what alternatives exist. Read this with your game country in mind.

The Dollar's Role Today

The US dollar is not just America's currency. It is the infrastructure of global trade. When a Thai manufacturer buys Brazilian soybeans, the transaction almost certainly clears through a US bank, in US dollars, over the SWIFT messaging network. The dollar serves three distinct functions in global finance:

Reserve Currency

Central banks hold dollar assets (primarily US Treasury bonds) as a store of value and emergency buffer. If a country needs to defend its own currency or pay for emergency imports, it sells Treasuries for cash.

Invoicing Currency

Most international commodities (oil, metals, grain) are priced in dollars regardless of who is buying or selling. A Saudi-Chinese oil transaction is quoted in dollars even though neither party uses the dollar domestically.

Settlement Medium

The actual payment infrastructure. SWIFT handles messaging; correspondent banks (almost always American) handle clearing. Over 88% of foreign exchange transactions involve the dollar on one side.

Safe Haven

In a global crisis, capital flows into dollars and Treasuries, not out. This "flight to safety" has historically made the dollar stronger during the moments when countries most need their reserves.

Why Countries Are De-dollarizing

De-dollarization does not mean the dollar becomes worthless. It means the dollar's dominance slowly erodes as countries build alternatives. Several forces are driving this shift simultaneously:

1. Sanctions as Financial Weapons

The most powerful accelerant. When Western nations froze roughly $300 billion in Russian central bank reserves in February 2022, every reserve manager on earth received the same message: dollar assets can be turned off. The reserves Russia thought it owned were, in practice, held in Western institutions subject to Western jurisdiction. China, with approximately $760 billion in Treasuries, watched carefully.

Key Concept: The Sanctions Paradox Sanctions work because the dollar is dominant. But each time sanctions are deployed, they incentivize targets and observers to diversify away from the dollar, weakening the very dominance that makes sanctions effective. The more the weapon is used, the less powerful it becomes.

2. Trade Pattern Shifts

China is now the largest trading partner of over 120 countries. If China accounts for 35% of your trade, holding 65% of your reserves in dollars and only 10% in yuan creates a mismatch between what you hold and what you need for settlement. As trade flows shift toward Asia, reserve composition logically follows.

3. US Fiscal Trajectory

US federal debt exceeded $36 trillion in 2025, with debt-to-GDP above 120%. While the US has never defaulted, periodic debt ceiling crises and rating downgrades (S&P in 2011, Fitch in 2023) create uncertainty for reserve managers who need their assets to be stable by definition.

4. Geopolitical Multipolarity

The dollar's reserve status was built in a unipolar world. As power distributes across multiple blocs (US-led, China-led, non-aligned), the monetary system faces pressure to match. BRICS nations collectively represent over 40% of global GDP at purchasing power parity.

The Alternatives

De-dollarization requires not just wanting to leave the dollar system but having somewhere else to go. Here is what currently exists:

AlternativeWhat It IsStrengthsWeaknesses
Chinese Yuan (CNY) China's currency, increasingly used in bilateral trade settlement via CIPS Backed by world's second-largest economy; growing trade settlement network; BRICS alignment Capital controls limit convertibility; China can devalue strategically; political risk of dependence on single partner
Gold Physical metal held in sovereign vaults No counterparty risk; impossible to freeze or sanction; 5,000-year track record as store of value Zero yield; difficult to liquidate quickly at scale; cannot be used directly for trade settlement
Bitcoin Decentralized digital asset with fixed supply Cannot be frozen by any government; borderless; limited supply creates scarcity value Extreme volatility (30-40% swings); almost no trade partners accept it; unproven at sovereign scale
Tokenized Treasuries US Treasury bonds represented as blockchain tokens Same yield as Treasuries; faster settlement; potentially tradeable 24/7 across borders Still denominated in dollars (sanctions-exposed); regulatory uncertainty; platform/smart contract risk
Tokenized Commodities Blockchain tokens backed by physical commodities (gold, oil) Combines commodity stability with blockchain settlement speed; potentially sanctions-resistant Custody and audit risk; thin markets; limited real-world settlement acceptance
BRICS Bridge / mBridge Alternative cross-border payment systems bypassing SWIFT Reduces dependence on Western payment infrastructure; enables direct bilateral settlement Early stage; limited liquidity compared to SWIFT; politically fragmented governance
The Tokenization Question The Reserve Currency Wargame groups all digital assets into one category. In reality, tokenized treasuries and Bitcoin have almost opposite risk profiles. Tokenized treasuries are dollarized, sanctionable, and yield-bearing. Bitcoin is decentralized, sanctions-proof, and volatile. Lumping them together masks fundamental differences. Your strategy memo should address this.

Alternative Payment Infrastructure

De-dollarization requires plumbing, not just politics. Two systems are challenging SWIFT's near-monopoly on cross-border payment messaging:

CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System)

China's alternative to SWIFT. Processed $24.5 trillion in transactions in 2024, up 43% year-over-year. Connects 1,400+ financial institutions across 110+ countries. Settles in yuan.

mBridge

BIS-backed CBDC bridge connecting central banks of China, Thailand, UAE, and Saudi Arabia. Uses distributed ledger technology for direct central bank-to-central bank settlement. Eliminates the need for US correspondent banks entirely. Still in pilot phase.

Timeline of Key Events

1944

Bretton Woods establishes dollar as global reserve currency, pegged to gold at $35/oz

1971

Nixon ends gold convertibility. Dollar floats freely but retains reserve status through oil pricing (petrodollar system)

2009

China launches cross-border yuan trade settlement pilot. PBOC Governor Zhou Xiaochuan proposes replacing the dollar with SDRs

2015

CIPS launches, giving China its own cross-border payment infrastructure independent of SWIFT

2022

Western nations freeze ~$300B in Russian central bank reserves. Global gold purchases surge. Saudi Arabia begins accepting yuan for some oil sales

2023

Central banks purchase 1,037 tonnes of gold (second-highest year on record). BRICS expands to include Saudi Arabia, UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran

2024

Dollar share of reserves falls to 57.8%, lowest since 1994. CIPS processes $24.5T in transactions (+43% YoY). Tokenized Treasury market exceeds $1B

Preparation Tip As you play, pay attention to the four metrics: growth, liquidity, safety, and settlement. They often conflict. A strategy that maximizes safety from sanctions (by holding gold and crypto) may devastate your settlement capability (because trade partners want dollars or yuan, not gold bars). The best strategies navigate these tensions rather than maximizing any single metric.
